Nebula

A nebula is not born — it is made. Forged, slowly and without intention, from the most primitive vocabulary the universe knows: fire, water, earth, wind. Four words. An infinite sentence.

It begins with a confrontation. Fire — restless, consuming, ancient — collides with water, its oldest adversary. They do not destroy each other. Instead, exhausted by the fight, they surrender into something neither could become alone: steam. Tension, resolved into softness.

Elsewhere, the earth does not sit still. Wind finds it, moves it, strips it down grain by grain until all that remains is dust — the humbled remains of something that once thought itself solid and permanent. That dust, given enough time and gravity, folds back into itself. It remembers what it was. It becomes a planet.

Two planets, drawn by forces they cannot name, find each other across the dark. Their meeting is neither gentle nor quiet — it is the kind of collision that produces light. That light is a star. Violent in its birth, breathtaking in its existence.

And then — steam meets star. The delicate meets the luminous. What was once a fight between fire and water, now wraps itself around a burning giant and unfurls across thousands of light-years in curtains of colour that no language was built to describe.

That is a nebula. Not found in some distant catalogue. Not handed down or stumbled upon. Built — deliberately, patiently — from nothing but the simplest things, combined in exactly the right order.
